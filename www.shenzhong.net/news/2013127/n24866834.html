How Much Gas Should I Buy This Week?

深圳中学 2013-01-27

                                                                  参与学生：唐维钊 李瑞鹏 何志扬 邰子正
                                                                   指导老师：鄢志俊
                                        该论文获得2012年美国中学生数学建模竞赛一等奖
 
 
 
 Abstract
 
In this paper, we undertake the gas purchasing problem. Our aim is to predict the trend of oil price development so as to decide how much oil to buy weekly to save money.
Noticing that there would be too many elements to put into account if we tried to build a model based on explanations of the factors of oil price, our team built a mathematical model based on analysis of data from the past with a program we wrote and improved it by optimizing and intelligentizing the algorithm we designed.
First, we built up a basic model using simple wavelet-like algorithm and static data (of Boston), which actually did not result in a satisfactory prediction.
On the basis of our first step, we furthered our model by making the algorithm more intelligent. The improved model was given the ability to self-study and to adjust dynamically while processing new data. This was a key step in the optimization, which dramatically raised the accuracy and precision of the prediction.
On our third step, we tried to find out details that might lead to inaccuracy of the model’s prediction, and amended them either by increasing the precision of the algorithm or by selecting another algorithm. From these changes we mainly gained marginal improvements.
Next, with the perfected model we made a prediction of the oil price each week in 2012. The prediction fitted the real figures satisfactorily, with an average relative deflection of less than 1.02% and a maximum of about 3.31%.
Finally, we designed a simple method based on the model that can help decide how much gas to buy each week in order that the user can save money spent on it.

1.     Restatement
Gasoline is perhaps one of the most important products constantly being produced and purchased in the international world. It affects politics, development of a country as well as the welfare of a family. As a result, the discussion about the varying price of gasoline has never stopped.
The money spent on gasoline in the U.S. mainly goes to four places. 60% to 70% is used to purchase crude oil, which makes up the most part. The other three elements are: refining costs and profits; distribution and marketing costs; taxes.
The main factors giving rise to or slowing down the variation in the price of gasoline can also be concluded:
Agitation:
1. Demand and supply: When the supply of oil cannot meet the growth in demand, the price rises. At times when the world economy developed, the demand grew. Signals in disasters cause rushes in the demand while conflicts, nuclear tensions disrupt the supply.
2. Refining process: The cost of refining process in different area varies, due to the technology available at the places. Other blended ingredients such as ethanol affect the refinery cost too.
Restriction:
1. Organizations: Organizations such as OPEC exert significant effect by setting up an upper limit on its members and making up standards.
2. Government: Federal government take actions on gasoline in order to affect its purchasing, maintain its stability, encourage choices on alternate-fueled vehicles, and so forth. These actions include imposing taxes, keeping emergency storage, etc.
 
 
2.     Analysis And Modeling
2.1. Basic Model
2.1.1. Analysis
In order to find possible mode of the oil price fluctuations, we firstly gave a close look at the weekly gasoline price charts and graphs given by EIA. We then focused on major and minor factors of gas price, including economic fluctuations, political events, wars, demand for oil, taxes, refining costs, weather, climate, vacations, and etc.
We found it too complicated to build an explanatory model taking into account all these factors. However, we did not think we could simply ignore some of them for simplification, as even an iota of change of one of the factors might influence the oil price significantly.
On the other hand, we had taken note of the fact that due to the existence of these factors, instead of changing randomly, oil price actually develops in some kind of patterns. That is, it is sort of going with “inertia”. Based on this point, we tried to build up a model which could work by analyzing data from the past so as to predict the future.
   
2.1.2. Modeling

2.2. Intelligentized Model
2.2.1. Analysis
As mentioned above in 2.1.3., we suppose the very inaccuracy and imprecision to be the result of errors gradually cumulated. To improve the situation, we optimize our model by enabling it to add processed data into its database, namely to self-study and self-upgrade. Also, every current mode is made up of real data collected from the last 3 weeks, and this is what we called dynamically adjusting.
 
2.2.2. Modeling
       On our first step, we enable our model to predict using latest data, which it adds into its database after processing them. Several changes are made in the program. Firstly, the data in array Data[] will be replaced with latest real data after storing all the predicted figures in another array, Backup[]. To realize this we add two sentences into the body of main loop, which means they are executed every time the program finishes making a prediction.
 
Backup[i - INDEX_2012] = Data[i];      // These two lines are essential to
Data[i] = DATA_2012[i - INDEX_2012]; // the model’s ability to self-upgrade
                                             // and to dynamically adjust
 
       Second, function FindMode() is also modified that it now searches for appropriate modes in all the data from the beginning to the very last week. This is realized by changing the terminal condition of the searching loop in FindMode().
 
void FindMode(int & nCurIndex)
{
    double A[3] = {0.0, Data[nCurIndex - 2] - Data[nCurIndex - 3], Data[nCurIndex - 1] - Data[nCurIndex - 3]};
    double B[3] = {0.0};

    int nMinMaxDiffIndex = 0;

We hypothesize that the errors are caused by flaws in both fitting and offsetting algorithms. In our next step, we will try to verify this hypothesis and further perfect the model.
 
2.3. Further Modified Model
2.3.1. Analysis
       In this step, we mainly locate two major flaws in the algorithms that might lead to inaccuracy.
       The first one is our fitting strategy. Currently, the only thing we put into account when measuring how good a mode fits another is the maximum absolute difference. However, we realize that only one way of determining the match degree may not be comprehensive enough. Therefore, we add another element into consideration: the convexity and concavity of a mode.
       Another one is the way we calculate the offset. Concerning the fact that modes are usually similar, not identical, our previous method of computing the offset might not be the most rational choice. Thus, we design a different way using second difference between the two modes.
 
2.3.2. Modeling
       Firstly, consider the convexity and concavity. It is obvious that a convex arc has a trend of going down more quickly or going up more slowly, whereas a concave arc has an opposite trend of going up more quickly or going down more slowly. Hence, even if two modes fit quite well regarding the maximum absolute difference, their trends might just be the opposites. Assuming that a convex mode can be used for the prediction of a concave one is illogical.
Therefore, we now take into account the convexity and concavity of the mode. We assume that a convex mode should not be used to fit a concave one, and vice versa.
       To realize this, we design a new function, Trend(), whose task is to tell whether a mode is a convex one or a concave one. This is done by comparing the slopes of line AB (or DE)and AC (or DF) as graph 2-3-1 shows:
                                                            
                                                 
                                                                      Graph 2-3-1
 
       The source code of Trend() is shown below:
 
// Input: mode to be judged
// Output: true if concave, false if convex
bool Trend(const double A[])
{
     if (A[2] - A[0] > 2.0 * (A[1] - A[0]))
        return true;
     else
         return false;
}
And with Trend() we can easily judge in FindMode() whether two modes are of the same convexity (or concavity). A new block of code calling Trend() is added to the function:
 
void FindMode(int & nCurIndex)

2.3.3. Reflection
       As shown above, we indeed managed to gain marginal improvements through detailed modifications. The improvement rate is not very high, but we suppose that we should try as many methods as possible to increase the accuracy and precision of the model, no matter how low the improvement rate is.
       On the other hand, although the fitting has been largely improved, there are still errors that we currently cannot correct. This might due to the small data scale and the disadvantage of this type of model itself. Also, we suppose this modeling method will work better on a continuous curve than on discrete points. Hence, we may consider dividing all the points into groups and connecting them all with smooth curves with equations in our further study.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
3.     Application
3.1. Analysis
In this part we use the model built and perfected earlier to help decide how to save money on gas.
In consideration of the fact that our model works very well for near-future predictions but poor for the farther ones, it is most accurate and precise to use it to predict the oil price of the following week. Therefore, long-term algorithms like dynamic programming cannot be applied to the work of deciding how much gas to buy. Instead, we use a greedy algorithm to solve the problem.
 
3.2. Strategy
Our rule is simple and easy to follow: every time before buying gas, use our model to predict the gas price of the next week. If the model indicates the price is going up the next week, buy as much oil as possible and fill up your tank; if the price is going down, buy half a tank of gas or no gas if possible. For specific purchasing plans see Appendix 4 (for case (I) below) and Appendix 5 (for case (II) below).
 
3.3. Validation
Following the rule in 3.2., we now calculate the expenses on gas for both case one and case two, and compare them to those if we buy regularly without a strategy:
 
(I) In the case that the user drives 200 miles per week, the cost of 2012 (until now) using this strategy is $1228.79, whereas the cost is $1288.82 if we buy half a tank of gas every week. The amount of money saved is $60.03.
(II) In the case that the user drives 100 miles per week, the cost of 2012 (until now) using this strategy is $583.55, whereas the cost is $645.53 if we buy half a tank of gas every two weeks. The amount of money saved is $61.98.
 
The above results prove that the greedy algorithm works well.
 
3.4. Limitation
       Although the greedy strategy works well for both case one and case two, it does have its limitation under some other circumstances: the vehicle uses more than half a tank of gas every week.
       The reason why the upper bound is half a tank is that if the vehicle uses more than half a tank of oil each week, its owner cannot choose to buy no gas (or even half a tank if it uses more than 3 / 4 tank each week). Consequently, our strategy will not work correctly in this situation.
 
 
 
 
 
 
4.     Discussion
Although our model successfully predict the oil price of 2012 with an average difference rate less than 1.02%, we still think there is a lot of space to improve.
Firstly, we found that our algorithm can actually be seen as a crude version of the wavelet algorithm. We suppose the latter one will have a better performance on analyzing data and predicting trends. However, the discrete data cannot be processed directly by the wavelet algorithm. We can firstly connect the discrete points with smooth curves using spline interpolation. In consideration of the fact that the interval between nearby items is only one week, cubic spline is appropriate.
Secondly, because we did not have plentiful data from the past, we could not ensure the accuracy of predictions farther than one week. In further study we will use much more data to train the model so that it will be able to make long-term predictions. This way we will be enabled to use dynamic programing to make an optimal arrangement that helps save gas cost significantly.
In our further study, we are going to optimize the model putting into account these elements.

// Input: mode to be judged
// Output: true if concave, false if convex
bool Trend(const double A[])
{
     if (A[2] - A[0] > 2.0 * (A[1] - A[0]))
        return true;
     else
         return false;
}
 
 
void FindMode(int & nCurIndex)

A letter to the local paper
 
Dear sir or madam:
 
We would like to thank you for paying attention to our result of the study on gas prices. In our study, we made attempt to predict the gas price by building up mathematic models. Our result is pleasing: By training our model only with data from 2011, we made a prediction of 2012 with an average relative deflection less than 1.02% and a maximum of 3.31%. We are sure it can be even more accurate and precise if provided with more data from a longer period.
The model is easy to use and trust-worthy. Every week before you decide how much gas to buy, you just need to provide the model with the today’s price, and then it can make its prediction of the next week based on it. The only thing you need to remember to do is provide it with the oil price every week. In this way, the accuracy and precision of the model can be better ensured.
But in the following two cases, the model might make wrong predictions:
 
1.              Our model works great on near-future predictions (one week for example). However, the result given by it for more than two weeks ahead is unreliable.
2.              Events like disaster, political conflict, economic crisis, etc., if unprecedented, can cause the model to make inaccurate predictions, but only for a short while. Our model can self-study and learn to adapt to the new situation.
 
We tried our model in two cases: using half tank of gas of a quarter tank of gas per week in the city of Boston. In the first case, the final cost for the first 44 weeks of 2012 is $1228.79, $60.03 less than that if buying half a tank each week. In case two the cost is $583.55, successfully saving $61.98. For its excellent performance, we strongly suggest that you consider introducing our model to the public.
